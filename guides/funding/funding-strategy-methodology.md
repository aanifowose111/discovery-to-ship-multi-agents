# Funding strategy methodology

How we decide **which funding path** makes sense for a given product, at a given stage. Funding is not a single thing — it is a menu of paths with different commitments, timelines, dilution implications, and exit expectations. Picking the wrong path is often more damaging than picking no path.

This is the first guide in the funding domain. The downstream guides (funding approach methodology, funding report methodology, planned `funding-pitch-reviewer`) will be picked based on the strategy chosen here.

---

## 1. Purpose

We will build a portfolio of products. Some will be cash-bootstrappable. Some will need outside capital. Some are categorically not VC-fundable but are excellent businesses. **The strategy methodology answers: for this product, at this stage, which funding path matches the economics, the founder's situation, and the realistic exit?**

Picking a funding path is a decision with carrying costs:

- VC money comes with growth expectations and a 7-10-year exit clock.
- Bootstrap requires founder runway and revenue discipline.
- Grants come with restricted use and reporting overhead.
- Crowdfunding commits you to a public-facing campaign and customer-as-investor relationships.
- Strategic investment can be cheap capital with expensive strings (right of first refusal, future competition risk).

The wrong path locks in the wrong constraints. The point of this methodology is to make the path choice deliberate.

---

## 2. Operating principles

1. **Bootstrap first if revenue is feasible.** Bootstrapping preserves optionality. You can always raise later; you cannot un-raise.
2. **Match the path to the product's economics, not the founder's ambition.** A lifestyle business funded by VC is a lifestyle business with mismatched investor expectations — a slow-motion failure. A high-growth opportunity funded only by bootstrapping may underinvest at the critical window.
3. **Funding is a milestone-purchase, not a generic resource.** You raise to hit a specific next milestone (PMF, first 1,000 customers, a specific hire, FDA filing, etc.). "More money to keep going" is a smell.
4. **Carrying costs are real.** Investor management, board meetings, reporting cadence, fiduciary duty, and the psychological weight of obligation all consume founder attention. Cheap capital with high carrying cost can be worse than no capital.
5. **The best time to raise is when you don't need to.** Raising from a position of forced runway is the weakest negotiating position.
6. **Most products do not need outside capital.** "Fundable" is a feature of certain businesses, not a goal in itself.
7. **Per the internet access policy in `CLAUDE.md`, web research happens by default** — investor profiles, grant programs, accelerator timing, comparable rounds.

## 3. The funding paths

A catalog of the paths we will consider, with first-order tradeoffs. Each path has its own downstream methodology guide (to be written) once it becomes the active strategy for a specific product.

### 3.1 Bootstrap (founder capital + revenue)

- **What it is:** Fund the build from savings; fund growth from product revenue.
- **Best for:** Products with short time-to-revenue, low fixed costs, and stable indie-scale unit economics.
- **Carrying cost:** Founder opportunity cost is the bill. No external reporting.
- **Risk:** Growth is rate-limited by revenue. Some windows close before bootstrap pace can catch them.

### 3.2 Revenue-based financing (RBF)

- **What it is:** Capital advanced against future revenue, repaid as a fixed percentage of revenue until a cap is hit.
- **Best for:** Already-revenue products that need a short capital injection (marketing spend, hiring) and want to avoid dilution.
- **Carrying cost:** Revenue share for a finite period. No equity.
- **Risk:** Cash-flow constraint during repayment. Mis-sized to a pre-revenue product, it is non-functional.

### 3.3 Friends & family

- **What it is:** Small amounts of equity (or convertible note) capital from people in your personal network.
- **Best for:** Bridging from "founder savings exhausted" to "first meaningful revenue or pre-seed."
- **Carrying cost:** Personal relationships at risk. Reporting is informal but emotionally weighted.
- **Risk:** Distorts personal relationships if the company fails. Requires the founder to have a network with the means.

### 3.4 Angel investment

- **What it is:** High-net-worth individuals investing $25k-250k typically, often industry-experienced.
- **Best for:** Pre-seed products that need ~$200k-$500k to validate or to hire a critical role, and where the angel adds expertise/network as well as money.
- **Carrying cost:** Light reporting (quarterly emails), but multiple angels means multiple relationships.
- **Risk:** Bad-fit angels can be more demanding than VCs at this scale.

### 3.5 Accelerators (YC, Techstars, Antler, etc.)

- **What it is:** Cohort programs offering a small standardized check (e.g., $500k SAFE), program structure, and a strong investor demo day.
- **Best for:** Founders who would benefit from peer cohort + structured program, and products with a believable VC-track future.
- **Carrying cost:** ~3-month time commitment, equity (~7%), program participation.
- **Risk:** YC and top-tier programs are very selective. Lower-tier programs may dilute without delivering proportionate value.

### 3.6 Pre-seed / seed VC

- **What it is:** Institutional venture capital, typically $500k-$5M at pre-seed, $2-15M at seed.
- **Best for:** Products with credible path to a $1B+ outcome, in markets where VC believes in the category.
- **Carrying cost:** Board meetings, formal reporting, growth-rate expectations, exit timeline, future-round dilution.
- **Risk:** Mismatched expectations are corrosive. Once on the VC track, "merely profitable" can look like failure.

### 3.7 Strategic investment / corporate venture

- **What it is:** Capital from a corporate buyer or its venture arm, often paired with a commercial agreement.
- **Best for:** Products where a specific corporate is a credible eventual acquirer or distribution partner.
- **Carrying cost:** Often includes right-of-first-refusal, exclusivity, or roadmap influence.
- **Risk:** Lock-in to one strategic. The strategic may compete with you or freeze your other corporate-customer pipeline.

### 3.8 Grants (research, government, foundation)

- **What it is:** Non-dilutive capital for specified work, often in regulated or research-adjacent categories.
- **Best for:** Products with a genuine research angle, or in sectors with active grant programs (health, education, defense, climate, AI safety). If the user's `user-context/INTERESTS.md` lists research-adjacent experience, lean into that touchpoint.
- **Carrying cost:** Application overhead is high. Reporting and use-restrictions are high. Grant deadlines do not flex.
- **Risk:** Pursuing grants that don't fit can consume months and produce nothing.

### 3.9 Crowdfunding (reward-based or equity)

- **What it is:** Reward-based (Kickstarter, Indiegogo) — pre-orders at scale. Equity (Wefunder, Republic, StartEngine) — small checks from many investors under Reg CF.
- **Best for:** Consumer products with a story, or community-driven products where the customer base is the investor base.
- **Carrying cost:** Public campaign + many-investor reporting. Reg CF has compliance overhead.
- **Risk:** Failed campaigns are public. Even successful ones consume significant founder attention during the campaign window.

### 3.10 Debt financing

- **What it is:** Bank loans, SBA loans, venture debt, lines of credit.
- **Best for:** Already-revenue products with predictable cash flow that need working capital or to extend runway between equity rounds.
- **Carrying cost:** Interest payments. Covenants. Personal guarantee likely at indie scale.
- **Risk:** Debt service in a downturn forces decisions that equity does not.

## 4. The funding decision framework

For a given product at a given stage, walk this framework in order. The first NO determines whether to pursue the path.

### 4.1 Stage check

What stage is the product at?

- **Pre-MVP** (just an idea or scoped brief): the only useful capital is to buy build time. Bootstrap or grants if applicable. Most other paths are premature.
- **Post-MVP, pre-revenue**: friends & family, angels, or accelerators if VC-track. Grants if research-aligned.
- **Post-revenue, pre-PMF** (revenue exists but retention/growth not yet proven): RBF if revenue is meaningful; bootstrap with discipline.
- **Post-PMF** (the product clearly works, growth has a slope): seed VC if VC-track and growth needs capital; bootstrap if margins are healthy; debt as a supplement.

### 4.2 Economics check

Is the product business VC-fundable on its economics?

- **Plausibly $1B+ TAM × $100M+ realistic revenue ceiling × > 100% YoY growth window** → VC-fundable on paper.
- **$10M-$100M realistic revenue ceiling × good margins × stable retention** → an excellent business, but a poor fit for traditional VC. Bootstrap, RBF, or strategic.
- **Lifestyle scale (sub-$10M)** → bootstrap or grants. Outside capital is mismatched.

If a product is not VC-fundable on economics, taking VC anyway is a strategy with a known failure mode.

### 4.3 Founder check

- **Risk tolerance for dilution.** A founder unwilling to give up >25% over the next 3 rounds should bias hard toward bootstrap / RBF / grants.
- **Time-budget.** Raising consumes founder time — typically 3-6 months per round at indie scale. Can the product survive that distraction?
- **Network access.** Some paths require pre-existing relationships (angels, accelerator referrers, grant officers). If access is zero, plan for the access-building work before the fundraising work.

### 4.4 Exit check

- **VC track requires a believable exit narrative** — acquisition by a strategic, IPO, or significant secondary. If the honest exit is "this becomes a sustainable indie business," VC will treat that as failure even if you treat it as success.
- **Grants require deliverables and often public-good outcomes** — not pure commercial activity.
- **Strategic investment narrows future exits** to or away from specific buyers.

### 4.5 Category check

- **Regulated industries** (health, education, defense, climate) often have grant programs designed for exactly your stage. Sweep them before assuming equity is the path.
- **Highly competitive consumer categories** require marketing capital that bootstrap rarely supplies — VC or debt becomes more necessary.
- **B2B SaaS with strong retention** is the canonical VC category, but also bootstrap-friendly at the indie scale we operate at.

## 5. The funding decision document

Every funding decision produces one file at `web-apps/<slug>/FUNDING.md` or `mobile-apps/<slug>/FUNDING.md` (colocated with the build), using this format:

```markdown
---
slug: <same slug as the product>
date-decided: YYYY-MM-DD
chosen-path: bootstrap | rbf | friends-family | angel | accelerator | seed-vc | strategic | grant | crowdfund | debt | hybrid
status: draft | active | revisit-pending | superseded
---

# Funding decision: <product name>

## Stage at decision
Pre-MVP / Post-MVP / Post-revenue / Post-PMF — with one paragraph of context.

## Capital need
Specific milestone the capital is for, with a number. "Six months runway plus one hire" with a dollar figure beats "we need money to grow."

## Path chosen
The path from §3, with a one-paragraph reason.

## Paths rejected and why
A short paragraph per other plausible path, naming why it does not fit. This is as important as the chosen path — it documents the decision so we don't relitigate in a weak moment.

## Carrying cost accepted
What this choice commits the founder to (reporting cadence, dilution, exit timeline, etc.).

## Trigger for revisit
What signal would warrant revisiting this decision? E.g., "revisit if revenue doesn't reach $X by date Y" or "revisit if a relevant accelerator batch opens."

## Open questions
Anything the methodology could not answer, kept visible until resolved.
```

The decision is **made by the user**. The main Claude drafts the document, surfaces tradeoffs, and points at the relevant downstream guide; the user signs.

## 6. Handoffs to downstream funding work

Once a path is chosen, the downstream work depends on the path:

| Chosen path | Next step (downstream guide / activity) |
|---|---|
| Bootstrap | Focus on revenue and unit economics. No funding-specific next step. |
| RBF | Identify 2-3 RBF providers (Pipe, Capchase, Founderpath, etc.), compare terms. Methodology guide for this: TBD. |
| Friends & family | Draft a one-page summary + a simple SAFE or convertible note. Methodology guide for this: TBD. |
| Angel | Investor research, outreach methodology, pitch materials. Methodology guide: TBD. The planned `funding-pitch-reviewer` reviews the pitch deck. |
| Accelerator | Application timing, applications to specific cohorts. Methodology guide: TBD. |
| Seed VC | Investor research, deck, financials, data room. Methodology guide: TBD. `funding-pitch-reviewer` again. |
| Strategic | Identify candidate strategics, term-sheet considerations. Methodology guide: TBD. |
| Grants | Sweep for fit grants, application calendar, application drafting. Methodology guide: TBD. |
| Crowdfunding | Platform choice (reward vs. equity), campaign methodology. Methodology guide: TBD. |
| Debt | Underwriting requirements per provider. Methodology guide: TBD. |

These downstream guides will be written when a real product picks a real path — there is no value in writing all ten now before any path is active.

## 7. When to revisit a funding decision

Revisit when **any** of these is true:

- The product reaches the trigger named in the decision document.
- The product stage shifts up or down (e.g., PMF reached, or growth stalls).
- A material trend finding (per `guides/market/trend-monitoring.md`) changes the category's funding climate — a hot category that goes cold, a regulator that opens or closes a grant program, an accelerator that opens a new vertical.
- The chosen path's carrying cost has become greater than predicted (e.g., investor relations is eating a third of founder time when ~10% was expected).
- 12 months have passed since the decision, regardless of cause — the climate has likely changed.

A revisit does not mean a change. It means re-running §4 against current state and signing the decision (the same path, or a new one) as the active strategy.
